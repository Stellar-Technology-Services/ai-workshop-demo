How Do I Use AI for Coding?

Mindset and workflow for a room that already ships with AI.

This isn't a sales pitch

  • You've done the intro training. AI is already a daily companion for review, docs, and debugging.
  • 100% of you verify before you ship. We are not going to relitigate whether you should.
  • So today isn't "should you" or "be careful." It's how strong users get stronger.

The gap isn't capability. It's the workflow and judgment that separate "the AI typed it" from "I directed it and I own it."

Survey: ~4/5 confidence, 100% verify, and "writing new code" sits at Weekly not Daily. That's a deliberate trust ceiling, not a skill gap. Naming it earns the room's attention and signals we read their input.

Manage AI like an intern

A brilliant, fast, eager intern who has never met your users.

  • 95%: researching, typing, boilerplate, first drafts. The intern is great here.
  • 5%: what to build, why, the constraints, the judgment call. That part is you.

Your job shifts from writing code to directing the work and owning the review.

The intern model is the spine of the session. Keep returning to it. It reframes "I didn't write this" anxiety into "I directed this and I'm accountable for it."

What's actually in the 5%

The intern can write almost anything. It still can't do these:

  • Knowing what not to build.
  • Domain knowledge it doesn't have: your users, your business rules, how claims actually work.
  • Judgment and taste: the tradeoff calls with no clean right answer.
  • Accountability: your name is on the commit, not the model's.

The model has the knowledge of the internet and the wisdom of none of it.

This slide is what stops the session feeling basic. The 5% is small in volume and huge in leverage. Everything downstream (scope, pushback, review) is just defending that 5%.

The 5% is the hard part

Prompting is useful. Understanding the problem and designing the solution is vital.

  • The bottleneck moved from syntax to specification.
  • Decompose first: what exactly are we building, what's in scope, what does "done" look like?
  • A clear brief beats a clever prompt every time.

If they remember one thing, it's this. It outlives any model or tool. The skill is decomposition, not phrasing.

Decomposition in practice

Vague

"Add a way to hide documents from the chat."

The model guesses the layer, sprawls across files, and you review a mess you didn't specify.

Decomposed

"Add an IncludedInRetrieval flag on Document, default true. Filter it in retrieval. Add a toggle in the UI. Preserve the flag when documents re-seed. Cover it with a test."

Bounded, reviewable, hard to get subtly wrong.

The decomposition is the work. The model fills it in.

This is the exact ticket we build live next session, so it doubles as a teaser. Point out: same feature, wildly different review burden, purely from how it was specified.

Right context, right time

The intern has no memory of your intent. The context window is all it knows right now. You are its memory.

  • Context is a budget, not a bucket. Irrelevant files don't just waste space, they pull the model off track.
  • Separate standing context (repo conventions, what's off-limits) from task context (these files, this ticket). Set the first once, supply the second per job.
  • Stale or contradictory context produces confident, wrong answers. Curate it like you'd curate a brief.
  • Long threads rot. When a session fills with dead ends, a fresh start with a tight brief beats nursing the wreck.

We'll set standing context with rules and instructions files in the next session.

Deeper than "point it at files." The real ideas: attention is a budget, you are the model's persistent memory, and standing vs task context is the distinction that pays off once you structure a repo (next session).

Push back, and override

Fluent is not the same as correct.

  • It will defend a bad approach as smoothly as a good one.
  • It agrees with you too easily. Agreement is not validation.
  • Your move: challenge assumptions, ask for alternatives and tradeoffs, make it justify the choice.
  • You are allowed to overrule it, and often you should.

A "pushback" skill packages this so it isn't just instinct.

Ties to the pushback skill we ship. The point: keep your own judgment in the loop. The model is a strong advisor, not the decision-maker.

Be the adult in the room

AI is great at writing code. It has no concept of scope creep.

  • It notices what's "missing," what "could" be nice, what it "could" refactor next door.
  • It optimizes for being helpful, not for shipping the smallest correct change.
  • Left alone, a one-line fix becomes a five-file detour.
  • Deciding what not to build is your job. Write down what's out of scope.

Next session the soft-delete ticket visibly balloons (add an audit log, bulk actions, a settings page) and we cut it back live. "Be the adult" is the line they'll remember.

Version hygiene, once generation is cheap

The old advice (commit often, small branches) still holds. Here's what actually changes with AI:

  • Review the diff, not the chat. The agent's summary is a claim. The diff is the truth.
  • Keep changes bisectable: a bad AI change should be one clean revert, not an archaeology dig.
  • Guard the irreversible actions. Force-pushes, history rewrites, hard resets, and committed secrets are hard or impossible to undo. Make the agent ask first.
  • Branches make experiments disposable: run an approach, keep the winner, throw the rest away.

"Review the diff, not the chat" is the keeper line. Reversibility and trust boundaries matter more than ever because the volume of generated change went up.

Make review repeatable

Today, review quality rides on one person's experience and memory.

  • That doesn't transfer: to teammates, to new hires, to the next team, or to you in six months.
  • Package the review instincts into shared, runnable standards so the review is the same no matter who runs it.
  • Teams differ. Each team should align on the skills and standards that fit their work. There is no one rule for everyone.

The best skills aren't generic. They carry the specifics of the work they're built for.

Dropped the "fork it, not a mandate" line (too on-the-nose). The keeper idea: a standard earns its keep by being specific to the team's actual work, not by being universal. Leadership wants consistency, the survey warned against one-size-fits-all, and specificity is how both hold.

Where fluent output still bites

The danger isn't bad code that looks bad. It's wrong code that reads perfectly.

StackCommon trap
C#/.NETInvented APIs, wrong overloads, dated patterns, async misuse
SQL/T-SQLDialect drift, N+1 from EF, queries that "work" but won't scale
VB.NET / legacyConfidently "modernizes" away behavior you actually needed

A veteran who knows this code catches these on sight. Everyone else has to dig in, and that uneven experience is the whole case for a shared review standard.

The framing point: experience is unevenly distributed. Long-tenured IFI devs spot these instantly, newer folks have to dig. That's the bridge back to "make review repeatable."
